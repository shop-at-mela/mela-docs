```
│ • Prefers fewer, higher-quality items over many cheap ones      │
│ • Shares finds with her parenting community                     │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ GOALS                                                           │
│ • Find baby products that are ACTUALLY sustainable              │
│ • Avoid mass-produced fast fashion for her kids                 │
│ • Support artisans and fair trade when possible                 │
│ • Discover unique products not available at Target/Amazon       │
│ • Feel good about her purchasing decisions                      │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ PAIN POINTS                                                     │
│ • "Every brand claims to be sustainable now"                    │
│ • "I can't tell what's real vs marketing"                       │
│ • "Etsy is hit or miss - quality varies wildly"                 │
│ • "I want to support small makers but discovery is hard"        │
│ • "International shipping usually means long waits and high $"  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ TRUST SIGNALS                                                   │
│ ✓ Third-party certifications (GOTS, OEKO-TEX, Fair Trade)      │

│ ✓ Real artisan photos (not stock images)                       │
│ ✓ Detailed material information                                │
│ ✓ Customer reviews mentioning quality                          │
│ ✗ Vague sustainability claims without proof                    │
│ ✗ "Eco-friendly" without specifics                             │
│ ✗ No information about who made the product                    │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ TECH COMFORT: ★★★★★ High                                        │
│ PRICE SENSITIVITY: ★★☆☆☆ Low (pays premium for quality)        │
│ PATIENCE: ★★★☆☆ Medium (will wait for right product)           │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Sarah's User Journey

1. **Discovery**: Searching "organic baby clothes not Amazon" or sees Instagram ad
2. **Evaluation**: Spends 10+ minutes reading brand story, checking certifications
3. **Comparison**: Opens multiple tabs, compares with other sustainable brands
4. **Decision**: Certification + artisan story + reviews = purchase
5. **Post-purchase**: Shares on social media if impressed, joins community

### Key Questions Sarah Asks

- "Who actually made this?"
- "What certifications do they have?"
- "Is this a real small brand or just marketing?"
- "What are the materials, specifically?"
- "Do other conscious parents recommend this?"

### Sarah's Relationship With "Indian" in Copy
Sarah is not Indian and does not identify with that label. She is shopping on Mela because of craft quality and genuine sustainability — not to collect "ethnic goods."
- **"Indian" on products** (e.g. "Made in Jaipur by artisans"): ✅ Signals authenticity and supply-chain transparency — exactly what she wants
- **"Indian" applied to her** (e.g. "your favourite Indian finds"): ❌ Reframes her as a cultural tourist, triggers subtle guilt about cultural appropriation — she never feels this on Etsy
- **Key copy principle**: Let the product description carry the cultural origin; let her personal layer be about her taste, not her ethnicity

- **Key copy principle**: Frame cultural context around occasions and product stories, not the user's identity

### Neha's User Journey

1. **Discovery**: Word of mouth in Indian mom groups, or searching for specific brand
2. **Evaluation**: Checks if her preferred brands are available, compares prices
3. **Validation**: Asks in WhatsApp group if others have ordered
4. **Decision**: If brand is known + price is fair + shipping is clear
5. **Post-purchase**: Recommends to her network if experience is good

### Key Questions Neha Asks

- "Do they have [specific brand] I know from India?"
- "What's the total cost including shipping?"
- "How much is this on Myntra/FirstCry?" (she WILL check)
- "Why is this ₹800 product selling for $40 here?"
- "How long will it actually take to arrive?"
- "Is this the same quality as buying in India?"
- "Have other people in my community ordered from here?"

### Neha's Relationship With "Indian" in Copy
Neha grew up in Chennai with a strong Tamil identity. "Indian" as a monolith is both accurate and reductive for her — it is true but it collapses Carnatic music, Kanchipuram silk, and Pongal traditions into the same bucket as Punjabi bhangra wear and Rajasthani block print.
- **"Indian" on products**: ✅ Acceptable at the macro level; she prefers more specific regional/brand attribution when possible ("Fabindia" or "Tirupur cotton" land better than "Indian cotton")
- **"Indian" applied to her collection**: ❌ Redundant and slightly reductive — she knows what she saved and why; the label adds nothing
- **Key copy principle**: Regional specificity beats national generality for trust. "Indian" is a floor, not a ceiling

## Cross-Cutting UXR Insight: The "Indian" Anchoring Principle

> *Updated: April 2026 — based on copy review of saved-items feature and deep persona analysis*

### The Three Layers — Only Two Are Healthy

There are three places "Indian" can appear in Mela's product experience. The first two create trust. The third causes harm across all four personas.

| Layer | Example | Effect |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **Product identity** | "Handwoven in Varanasi" / "GOTS certified, made in India" | ✅ Accurate, trust-building, always use |
| **Brand identity** | "Mela" (Hindi: gathering/fair) / "100+ Indian brands" | ✅ Brand carries cultural meaning so copy doesn't have to |
| **User identity** | "your favourite Indian finds" / "for Indian parents" | ❌ Reduces user to their ethnicity — harmful for all 4 personas differently |

### Why This Matters for Each Persona

- **Sarah**: "Indian finds" reframes her as a cultural tourist buying ethnic goods. She shops for quality and craft; the label implies her taste is about identity, not values.
- **Priya**: Her children are mixed-heritage. "Indian finds" creates an invisible wall when browsing with Matt or sharing items with non-Indian friends. Her cultural choices are part of her whole family life, not a separate ethnic bucket.
- **Neha**: "Indian" as a monolith flattens her Tamil/South Indian identity. She's not saving generic "Indian things" — she's saving specific brands she trusts from specific regions.
- **Arun**: He experiences impostor syndrome about not being "Indian enough." UI copy that labels his collection as "Indian finds" quietly scores his performance of Indian-ness on every hover interaction.

### The Rule

**"Mela" carries the cultural identity so copy doesn't have to.**

Every user who reaches the platform has already passed through the cultural frame — the brand name, the product catalog, the artisan stories all signal Indian design. The user's personal experience layer (saved items, collection, personal copy) should be about *their taste*, not their ethnicity.

| Copy context | Use "Indian"? | Use instead |
|-------------|--------------|------------|
| Product origin description | ✅ Always | "Handwoven in Rajasthan" / "Made by artisans in Tirupur" |
| Brand-level catalog description | ✅ Yes | "100+ Indian brands curated for you" |
| Category navigation labels | ✅ Yes | "Indian Fashion" / "Indian Baby" |
| User possessives / personal layer | ❌ Never | "your favourite Mela finds" / "your collection" |
| Persona-targeted campaigns | ⚠️ Sparingly | Only when Indian-American identity IS the explicit context (Diwali guides, heritage gifting) |
| Cultural texture / sub-labels | ✅ Yes (opt-in) | "meri pasand" as italic subtitle, not primary nav label |

### Applied Example: Saved Items Tooltip

| Version | Assessment |
|---------|------------|
| `"Meri Pasand — your favourite Indian finds"` | ❌ Double problem: Hindi jargon primary + "Indian" labels the user |
| `"Meri Pasand — your favourite Mela finds"` | ⚠️ Better, but "Meri Pasand" is still primary (Hindi barrier for Sarah, Arun) |
| `"Saved — your favourite Mela finds"` | ✅ Functional English primary, brand carries cultural identity |
| `"Save to your collection"` | ✅ Best for universal clarity — cultural texture lives in the icon design, not the label |
